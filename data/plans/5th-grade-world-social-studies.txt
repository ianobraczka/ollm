==================================================

Grade: 5

Subject: World Social Studies

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Fifth-grade World Social Studies at Colégio Horizonte introduces students to the foundations of human civilization through ancient societies, geographic thinking, and historical inquiry. Students examine how early peoples organized around rivers, trade routes, and shared beliefs; compare governance and daily life in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, and Rome; and explore achievements of civilizations in Asia, Africa, and the Americas. Map skills, timelines, and primary-source analysis follow C3 Framework inquiry habits—asking questions, evaluating evidence, and considering multiple perspectives—while BNCC História competencies anchor content in world history standards. Light connections to Brazil appear when comparing indigenous technologies, trade goods, and heritage preservation with global examples students encounter in Rio de Janeiro museums and cultural sites.

Core Competencies:
- Use maps, timelines, and geographic tools to organize information about ancient societies
- Compare political, economic, and cultural features of major early civilizations
- Analyze primary and secondary sources to distinguish evidence from interpretation
- Connect concepts of citizenship, diversity, and heritage to historical and contemporary examples

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
Early civilizations emerged where geography, agriculture, and shared institutions allowed complex societies to develop.

Topics:
- Defining civilization: cities, writing, specialized labor, and organized religion
- Mesopotamia and the Fertile Crescent: ziggurats, cuneiform, and Hammurabi's Code
- Ancient Egypt: Nile River geography, pharaohs, pyramids, and hieroglyphics
- World map and regional map skills: continents, oceans, hemispheres, and scale
- Timelines, periodization, and marking the passage of time across cultures
- Introduction to historical inquiry: compelling questions, sources, and evidence

Learning Objectives:
- Explain how river valleys supported the rise of Mesopotamian and Egyptian civilizations
- Locate major ancient regions on world and regional maps using legends and coordinates
- Construct and read timelines showing sequence, duration, and overlap of events
- Distinguish primary from secondary sources using artifacts, images, and texts
- Compare how Mesopotamian and Egyptian societies organized power and daily life
- Formulate investigable questions about how environment shapes human settlement

BNCC Skills:
- EF05HI01, EF05HI08, EF06HI05, EF06HI07

Keywords:
- civilization

- Mesopotamia
- Egypt
- maps
- inquiry
- sources

Project Opportunities:
- Nile and Tigris-Euphrates comparison map with labeled geographic advantages
- Artifact analysis station: replica cuneiform tablet or Egyptian amulet with observation cards
- Classroom timeline mural from prehistory through early civilizations with scale markers

Assessment Suggestions:
- Map labeling quiz with short written explanations of geographic influence
- Source sort activity: primary vs. secondary with justification for each item
- Written response comparing one feature of Mesopotamian and Egyptian governance

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
Classical Greece and Rome developed ideas about law, citizenship, and empire that continue to shape governments and cultures worldwide.

Topics:
- Ancient Greece: city-states, democracy in Athens, and cultural achievements
- Greek contributions to philosophy, theater, Olympic tradition, and architecture
- Roman Republic and Empire: law, roads, aqueducts, and territorial expansion
- Concepts of citizenship, rights, and responsibilities in classical societies
- Comparisons with civic life in Brazil: voting, public spaces, and rule of law
- Evaluating multiple perspectives on empire: builders, subjects, and enslaved people

Learning Objectives:
- Describe key features of Athenian democracy and Roman republican government
- Identify Greek and Roman contributions to law, engineering, and the arts
- Explain how geography influenced Greek trade and Roman military expansion
- Compare inclusion and exclusion in classical citizenship with modern democratic ideals
- Analyze historical images and texts for author purpose and point of view
- Connect Roman infrastructure concepts to contemporary urban systems in Rio de Janeiro

- Comparison chart of Athenian democracy and Roman republican institutions
- Document-based question using a short excerpt from Roman law or Greek philosophy

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
Diverse ancient societies across Asia, Africa, and the Americas developed sophisticated cultures adapted to distinct environments and trade networks.

Topics:
- Ancient China: dynasties, writing, silk, and the Great Wall at introductory level
- Ancient India: Indus Valley, Hindu and Buddhist traditions, and mathematical advances
- African kingdoms and empires: Mali, trade across the Sahara, and Timbuktu as a learning center
- Pre-Columbian Americas: Maya, Aztec, and Inca achievements in agriculture, astronomy, and architecture
- Trade routes connecting continents: Silk Road, trans-Saharan exchange, and Pacific coastal networks
- Connections to Brazil: comparing indigenous technologies and oral traditions with global examples

Learning Objectives:
- Locate major ancient societies of Asia, Africa, and the Americas on world maps
- Describe economic and cultural exchanges enabled by long-distance trade routes
- Compare how different civilizations adapted to mountains, deserts, and tropical environments
- Recognize contributions of non-European societies to science, art, and governance
- Evaluate maps and illustrations of trade routes for accuracy and bias
- Discuss how cultural diffusion enriches societies while raising questions about power and contact

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
Studying ancient world heritage through inquiry prepares students to protect memory, evaluate claims, and participate as informed citizens.

Topics:
- World heritage sites and UNESCO concepts: preservation, memory, and shared human patrimony
- Archaeology and historiography: how historians build knowledge from fragments and debate
- Comparing ancient and modern issues: migration, labor, inequality, and cultural contact
- Human rights foundations: dignity, slavery in the ancient world, and evolving moral standards
- C3-style inquiry cycle: questioning, research, argument, and informed action
- Integration review: geography skills, civilizations, sources, and citizenship connections

Learning Objectives:
- Inventory local and world heritage examples and explain why they merit protection
- Apply the inquiry process to investigate a question about one ancient civilization
- Compare ancient and contemporary viewpoints on a shared human issue such as migration
- Analyze who is represented or omitted in museum displays and textbook narratives
- Synthesize map, timeline, and source skills across units in a capstone investigation
- Propose a student action that promotes respect for cultural heritage in the community

- Capstone inquiry poster: question, sources, claim, evidence, and suggested action
- End-of-year concept map connecting geography, civilizations, trade, and citizenship themes

Assessment Suggestions:
- Inquiry portfolio evaluated with C3-style rubric: question, sources, argument, and communication
- Heritage inventory with justification for local and world selections
- Final reflection on how studying ancient worlds informs responsible citizenship today
