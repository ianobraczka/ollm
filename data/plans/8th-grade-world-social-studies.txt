==================================================

Grade: 8

Subject: World Social Studies

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Eighth-grade World Social Studies at Colégio Horizonte examines the Cold War, decolonization, globalization, and contemporary human rights challenges that define the world students inherit. Students analyze superpower rivalry, proxy conflicts, and nuclear tension alongside liberation movements in Africa and Asia. The course explores how trade, technology, and migration connect societies—and how inequality, terrorism, and identity politics generate debate about global justice. BNCC História codes anchor the sequence while C3 Framework inquiry trains students to evaluate media sources, compare policy arguments, and propose informed civic responses. Brazil provides recurring context through the Cold War dictatorship era, Mercosur and BRICS participation, digital citizenship, and Brazil's role in multilateral forums.

Core Competencies:
- Analyze Cold War ideologies, conflicts, and their impact on regions including Latin America
- Explain decolonization and independence movements in Africa and Asia after 1945
- Evaluate benefits and critiques of globalization in economic, cultural, and technological dimensions
- Apply human rights frameworks to contemporary issues using evidence and multiple perspectives

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
The Cold War divided the world into competing blocs whose ideological rivalry shaped politics, culture, and conflict from 1945 through 1991.

Topics:
- Origins of the Cold War: Yalta and Potsdam, Iron Curtain, containment, and Marshall Plan
- NATO and Warsaw Pact; nuclear arms race and deterrence at introductory level
- Proxy wars and crises: Korea, Vietnam, Cuban Missile Crisis, and Berlin
- Non-aligned movement and Third World perspectives on superpower competition
- Cold War in Latin America: US-Soviet influence, coups, and dictatorships
- Brazil during the Cold War: 1964 coup, military regime, and redemocratization overview

Learning Objectives:
- Define Cold War and explain why direct US-Soviet war was avoided despite intense rivalry
- Map allied and contested regions during key Cold War phases
- Compare American and Soviet claims about freedom, security, and development with critical sourcing
- Describe how proxy conflicts affected civilians in Korea, Vietnam, and Latin America
- Analyze primary sources such as speeches, propaganda posters, and declassified memos
- Formulate questions about how ideological competition limited or distorted self-determination

- EF08HI28, EF08HI29, EF08HI30

Keywords:
- Cold War

- ideology
- proxy war
- Latin America
- Brazil
- containment

Project Opportunities:
- Cold War crisis simulation with sourced briefing cards and debrief on human costs
- Propaganda poster analysis from US, Soviet, and non-aligned perspectives
- Timeline linking global Cold War events to Brazilian political milestones 1964–1985

Assessment Suggestions:
- Map and identification test on alliances, crises, and bloc leadership
- Document-based question comparing Truman Doctrine and Soviet response documents
- Structured essay on how Cold War rivalry affected one Latin American country

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
Decolonization reshaped the global map as African and Asian peoples challenged European empires and asserted new nations with diverse political paths.

Topics:
- End of European empires: India, Ghana, Algeria, and Indonesia as case studies
- Pan-Africanism, nationalism, and leadership figures such as Gandhi, Nkrumah, and Mandela overview
- Challenges of new nations: borders, ethnic diversity, economic dependency, and Cold War alignment
- Apartheid in South Africa and the long struggle for equality
- Portuguese decolonization and its ties to Brazilian historical memory
- Comparing independence processes in Africa and Asia with Latin American earlier experiences

Learning Objectives:
- Explain economic and political factors that weakened European colonial control after WWII
- Compare independence strategies: nonviolent resistance, armed struggle, and negotiated transition
- Analyze how arbitrary colonial borders created ongoing conflicts in postcolonial states
- Describe apartheid's policies and the international movement that supported abolition
- Evaluate primary sources from independence leaders for goals, methods, and limitations
- Discuss Brazil's connections to Lusophone Africa through language, migration, and diplomacy

- Short essay on how Cold War competition influenced postcolonial alignments

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
Globalization connects economies, cultures, and technologies across borders—creating opportunity for some while prompting critical movements that question power and fairness.

Topics:
- Defining globalization: trade liberalization, multinational corporations, and supply chains
- International institutions: WTO, IMF, World Bank, and regional blocs including Mercosur
- Cultural exchange, migration, and diaspora communities in contemporary cities
- Digital revolution: internet, social media, and transformations in politics and work
- Environmental globalization: climate change, resource consumption, and shared responsibility
- Brazil in the global economy: commodities, industry, BRICS membership, and urban inequality

Learning Objectives:
- Explain how trade, finance, and communication technologies accelerate global interdependence
- Interpret graphs and maps showing trade flows, migration, and foreign investment patterns
- Compare arguments for and against globalization using evidence from workers, consumers, and activists
- Analyze how digital tools both democratize information and enable misinformation
- Connect Rio de Janeiro's service and tourism economy to global networks
- Evaluate sources for bias when researching multinational companies or protest movements

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
Human rights, identity, and security in the twenty-first century require citizens who can analyze conflict, defend dignity, and act on evidence in a connected world.

Topics:
- Universal human rights frameworks revisited: civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights
- Contemporary challenges: terrorism, migration crises, and tensions over cultural difference
- Identity, diversity, and inclusion in global societies; combating prejudice and violence
- International humanitarian law, refugees, and Brazil's reception policies in comparative context
- Sustainable development goals and youth civic participation
- Capstone C3 inquiry: researching a global issue and proposing informed local action

Learning Objectives:
- Apply human rights language to contemporary scenarios involving speech, security, and migration
- Analyze causes and consequences of terrorism without stereotyping religions or national groups
- Compare how different countries balance security and rights during crises
- Evaluate Brazil's 1988 Constitution and international commitments as human rights instruments
- Synthesize Cold War, decolonization, and globalization themes in a culminating investigation
- Design a feasible action plan addressing a global-local issue such as climate justice or refugee support

Assessment Suggestions:

- Capstone inquiry portfolio with question, sources, argument, counterargument, and action plan
- Final reflection essay connecting eighth-grade world studies to responsible global citizenship
