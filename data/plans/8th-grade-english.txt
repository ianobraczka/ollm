==================================================

Grade: 8

Subject: English

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Eighth-grade English at Colégio Horizonte prepares students for advanced secondary study through Shakespeare introduction, American literature selections, and formal debate. Students read challenging texts with historical and cultural context, analyze argumentation, and defend positions orally and in writing. Near-native command of English supports rigorous discussion of canonical and contemporary works, with thoughtful connections to Brazilian students' multilingual identities.

Core Competencies:
- Read and interpret Shakespearean language with scaffolded support and performance
- Analyze themes and contexts in selected American literary texts
- Construct and refute arguments in structured academic debate
- Produce polished argumentative writing with claims, evidence, and counterclaims

==================================================

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
Shakespeare's language and drama remain accessible when readers combine close reading, performance, and historical context.

Topics:
- Elizabethan context: theatre, society, and language change over time
- Shakespeare introduction: selected scenes from A Midsummer Night's Dream or Romeo and Juliet
- Blank verse, iambic pentameter, and paraphrasing archaic diction
- Dramatic structure: acts, scenes, soliloquy, and aside
- Performance-based learning: tableaux, scene staging, and oral interpretation

Learning Objectives:
- Paraphrase Shakespearean lines into contemporary English accurately
- Identify dramatic conventions and explain their function in a scene
- Analyze a character's motivation using dialogue and stage directions
- Perform a scene with attention to meaning, not mere memorization
- Connect themes of love, authority, or identity to modern adolescent experience

BNCC Skills:
- EF08LI03, EF08LI05, EF08LI06, EF08LI07, EF08LI18

Keywords:
- Shakespeare
- drama
- paraphrase
- performance
- Elizabethan context
- close reading

Project Opportunities:
- Scene performance with director's notebook explaining staging choices
- Shakespeare language translation journal: original line, paraphrase, modern rewrite
- Elizabethan research mini-poster linking history to plot events

Assessment Suggestions:
- Paraphrase quiz on assigned scenes with short rationale
- Performance rubric for clarity, interpretation, and collaboration
- Character motivation paragraph citing Shakespearean dialogue

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
American literature reflects diverse voices and historical moments that shape identity, conflict, and the American experience.

Topics:
- Selected short fiction and poetry from American authors (e.g., Hurston, Hughes, Bradbury excerpts)
- Historical context: migration, civil rights, and regional landscapes
- Symbolism and allegory in American texts
- The American Dream as a recurring theme: critique and reinvention
- Comparison with Brazilian literary themes of belonging and social change

Learning Objectives:
- Place a text in historical context and explain how context shapes meaning
- Analyze symbolism and recurring motifs in American short fiction or poetry
- Compare thematic concerns across American and Brazilian perspectives
- Write analytical paragraphs integrating historical background and quotation
- Lead small-group discussions with prepared questions and textual references

BNCC Skills:
- EF08LI04, EF08LI08, EF08LI09, EF08LI10, EF08LI13

Keywords:
- American literature
- historical context
- symbolism
- theme
- diversity
- analysis

Project Opportunities:
- American author study with timeline and thematic one-pager
- Cross-cultural comparison essay: American and Brazilian texts on belonging
- Found poetry or blackout poetry from a studied American poem

Assessment Suggestions:
- Thematic analysis essay on an American text with contextual introduction
- Symbol tracking chart with culminating interpretive paragraph
- Discussion leadership evaluation with peer feedback

==================================================

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
Debate develops critical thinking when speakers construct claims, support them with evidence, and respond to opposition respectfully.

Topics:
- Argument structure: claim, warrant, evidence, and rebuttal
- Logical fallacies: ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, and appeal to emotion
- Formal debate formats: Lincoln-Douglas introduction and team parliamentary basics
- Research for debate: note-taking, source credibility, and citation
- Topics connected to literature and contemporary Rio/global issues (e.g., censorship, environment, technology)

Learning Objectives:
- Distinguish claim, evidence, and reasoning in spoken and written arguments
- Identify common fallacies in sample arguments and explain why they weaken logic
- Prepare affirmative and negative cases on a assigned resolution
- Deliver rebuttal that addresses opponents' strongest points, not straw men
- Evaluate debates using a rubric focused on evidence, clarity, and respect

BNCC Skills:
- EF08LI01, EF08LI02, EF08LI11, EF08LI14, EF08LI15

Keywords:
- debate
- argumentation
- rebuttal
- fallacies
- evidence
- persuasion

Project Opportunities:
- Class debate tournament on a literature-linked resolution
- Fallacy detection workshop using media clips and op-ed samples
- Debate brief portfolio with bibliography and speaking notes

Assessment Suggestions:
- Debate performance rubric: construction, delivery, rebuttal, and civility
- Written argument outline with claim, evidence, and anticipated counterarguments
- Fallacy identification test with short explanations

==================================================

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
Eighth graders integrate literary analysis, cultural knowledge, and argumentative skill to demonstrate readiness for secondary English.

Topics:
- Argumentative essay mastery: introduction, body, counterclaim, and conclusion
- Comparative literary essay: Shakespeare, American text, or debate topic synthesis
- Future tense and modality in English for plans, predictions, and recommendations
- Media literacy: persuasive techniques in news, advertising, and social platforms
- Capstone presentation: defended literary or civic argument with multimedia support

Learning Objectives:
- Write argumentative essays with counterclaim paragraph and rebuttal
- Compare texts or ideas in a sustained comparative essay
- Use future forms and modals accurately in proposals and predictions
- Analyze persuasive techniques in media texts critically
- Deliver capstone presentation synthesizing a year's learning with Q&A defense

BNCC Skills:
- EF08LI12, EF08LI16, EF08LI17, EF08LI19, EF08LI20 (reinforcement and integration)

Keywords:
- argumentative writing
- counterclaim
- comparative essay
- media literacy
- capstone
- secondary readiness

Project Opportunities:
- Argumentative essay on a school or community issue with researched evidence
- Capstone multimedia presentation linking literature to a personal civic stance
- Media analysis project: deconstruct persuasion in English-language campaigns

Assessment Suggestions:
- Full argumentative essay rubric including counterclaim and rebuttal
- Comparative literary essay on two texts studied during the year
- Capstone defense evaluated for argument quality, delivery, and synthesis
