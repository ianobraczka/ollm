==================================================

Grade: 6

Subject: World Social Studies

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Sixth-grade World Social Studies at Colégio Horizonte examines the medieval world, Renaissance transformations, and the age of exploration that reshaped global connections. Students analyze feudal structures in Europe, intellectual and commercial achievements in the Islamic world and Africa, and the humanist revival that questioned medieval authority. The year culminates in study of maritime exploration, transoceanic exchange, and the Columbian Exchange—always through inquiry into causes, consequences, and competing perspectives. BNCC História codes guide content selection while C3 Framework habits emphasize sourcing, contextualization, and argument from evidence. Brazil enters the narrative naturally through Portugal's maritime school, the Treaty of Tordesillas, and early comparisons between European claims and existing American societies.

Core Competencies:
- Explain how medieval and early modern societies organized power, faith, and trade
- Analyze Renaissance and Reformation ideas as engines of cultural and political change
- Evaluate causes and consequences of European exploration and global exchange
- Use maps, documents, and multiple perspectives to construct evidence-based arguments

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
Medieval societies in Europe, the Islamic world, and Africa developed interconnected systems of faith, trade, and governance that bridged ancient and modern eras.

Topics:
- Defining the Middle Ages: feudalism, manorialism, and the role of the Catholic Church in Europe
- Medieval daily life: peasants, knights, guilds, and urban growth in European towns
- The Islamic Golden Age: scholarship, medicine, mathematics, and preservation of classical knowledge
- Sub-Saharan trade networks: gold, salt, and the learning centers of West Africa

Learning Objectives:
- Describe basic features of European feudal society and the Church's institutional power
- Explain how Islamic scholars advanced science, navigation, and philosophy
- Map major medieval trade routes connecting Europe, Africa, and Asia
- Compare social roles and rights across medieval European, Islamic, and African contexts
- Identify causes of urban and commercial growth in selected medieval centers
- Formulate questions about how trade spreads ideas as well as goods

Keywords:
- Middle Ages

- trade
- Africa
- continuity
- change

Project Opportunities:
- Medieval trade route map with symbols for goods, ideas, and cultural exchanges
- Comparative chart of daily life in a European manor, Islamic city, and West African market
- Document analysis of a medieval travel account or commercial record with sourcing questions

Assessment Suggestions:
- Map and vocabulary test on medieval regions, routes, and institutions
- Written comparison of two medieval societies' approaches to learning and trade
- Source-based short answer explaining how the Islamic world connected classical and Renaissance knowledge

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
The Renaissance and Reformation reshaped European thought, art, and religion by reviving classical learning and challenging unified spiritual authority.

Topics:
- Humanism and Renaissance art: perspective, patronage, and rediscovery of classical texts
- Key figures and city-states: Florence, Venice, and the role of printing
- Protestant and Catholic Reformations: Luther, religious conflict, and literacy
- Scientific curiosity and exploration-minded culture in early modern Europe

Learning Objectives:
- Define humanism and identify its influence on art, education, and politics
- Explain how the printing press accelerated religious and scientific debate
- Describe causes of the Protestant Reformation and the Catholic response
- Analyze a Renaissance artwork or text for values, audience, and historical context
- Compare Renaissance ideals of the individual with medieval corporate identity
- Discuss how cultural renewal can both liberate and divide societies

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI01, EF06HI04, EF06HI05

Keywords:
- Renaissance
- humanism
- Reformation
- art
- printing
- religion
- modernity

Project Opportunities:
- Renaissance gallery: student reproductions of artworks with analysis cards on technique and message
- Reformation debate using sourced speech cards from reformers, monarchs, and ordinary believers
- Timeline of ideas linking classical revival to exploration and scientific inquiry

Assessment Suggestions:
- Document-based question on a Renaissance humanist excerpt or Reformation pamphlet
- Visual analysis worksheet comparing two artworks for perspective, symbolism, and patron influence
- Quiz on key vocabulary: humanism, indulgence, sect, patronage, and literacy

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
European maritime exploration, driven by technology, commerce, and competition, opened oceanic routes that connected continents in unprecedented and unequal ways.

Topics:
- Motives for exploration: spices, gold, glory, and Christian expansion
- Navigation advances: caravels, astrolabe, maps, and Portuguese and Spanish leadership
- Voyages of Columbus, da Gama, Magellan, and Cabral; circumnavigation and route mapping
- Treaty of Tordesillas and division of claimed territories; Brazil within Portuguese sphere

Learning Objectives:
- Explain technological and economic factors that enabled transoceanic voyages
- Map major exploration routes and identify regions reached by European navigators
- Describe the Treaty of Tordesillas and its significance for South American colonization
- Compare stated motives of explorers with outcomes for indigenous and African peoples
- Analyze primary sources such as logs, letters, and maps for perspective and purpose
- Construct an evidence-based claim about whether exploration primarily benefited or harmed world societies

Keywords:

Assessment Suggestions:
- Route map examination with written analysis of one voyage's global significance
- Structured debate rubric scoring use of evidence on exploration costs and benefits

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
The Columbian Exchange transformed diets, economies, and populations worldwide, requiring historians to trace long-term consequences from multiple viewpoints.

Topics:
- Columbian Exchange: crops, animals, diseases, and technologies crossing the Atlantic
- Impact on Europe, Africa, and the Americas: demography, agriculture, and labor systems
- Early contact societies in the Americas and African societies before intensified enslavement
- Mercantilism and early colonial economies; introduction to modern slavery's distinct features

Learning Objectives:
- List major biological and cultural exchanges of the Columbian Exchange with regional effects
- Explain how disease and displacement devastated many indigenous American populations
- Describe how demand for labor linked exploration to the transatlantic slave trade
- Compare European, indigenous, and African perspectives on early contact events
- Evaluate charts and maps showing commodity flows for accuracy and missing voices
- Propose a community-aware response to a contemporary issue rooted in colonial exchange history

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI02, EF06HI08, EF06HI15, EF06HI16, EF06HI17

Keywords:
- Columbian Exchange
- contact
- slavery
- mercantilism
- perspectives
- consequences

Project Opportunities:
- Before-and-after world diet map showing global spread of maize, potatoes, sugar, and coffee
- Triangular trade simulation with reflection on ethical limits of role-play
- Capstone research display: one exchanged item traced from 1500 to present-day Brazil or Rio

Assessment Suggestions:
- Cause-and-effect diagram linking exploration to exchange, labor systems, and demographic change
- Multi-perspective written account of first contact from three sourced viewpoints
- End-of-year inquiry report with claim, evidence, counterclaim, and action recommendation
