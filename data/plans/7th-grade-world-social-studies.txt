==================================================

Grade: 7

Subject: World Social Studies

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Seventh-grade World Social Studies at Colégio Horizonte traces the modern world from Enlightenment thought through industrialization, nationalism, and the global wars of the twentieth century. Students examine how revolutions in America and France challenged absolute rule, how factories and railroads transformed work and cities, and how imperial competition and alliance systems led to world conflict. The year introduces the Holocaust, the founding of the United Nations, and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights as foundations for understanding atrocity prevention and international cooperation. BNCC História competencies structure the curriculum; C3 Framework inquiry pushes students to evaluate sources, compare interpretations, and connect past decisions to present civic responsibilities. Brazil appears through coffee industrialization, World War II participation, and refugee and migration comparisons.

Core Competencies:
- Analyze Enlightenment and revolutionary ideas as drivers of political and social change
- Explain how industrialization reshaped economies, labor, and urban life worldwide
- Evaluate causes, conduct, and consequences of World War I and World War II
- Use historical evidence to discuss human rights, genocide prevention, and international institutions

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
Enlightenment ideas about reason, rights, and representation fueled revolutions that redefined the relationship between citizens and government.

Topics:
- Enlightenment thinkers: Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, and Voltaire at accessible level
- American Revolution: colonists' grievances, independence, and constitutional principles
- French Revolution: estates, Declaration of the Rights of Man, Terror, and Napoleonic era overview
- Comparing revolutionary outcomes: republics, constitutions, and limits on participation
- Latin American independence movements in broad context; connections to Atlantic revolutions
- Source work: political cartoons, declarations, and pamphlets as evidence of competing ideals

Learning Objectives:
- Summarize key Enlightenment ideas about natural rights, separation of powers, and popular sovereignty
- Compare causes and results of the American and French Revolutions using a structured framework
- Explain how revolutionary language included and excluded women, enslaved people, and the poor
- Analyze primary sources for argument, audience, and historical context
- Identify links between Atlantic revolutions and independence struggles in the Americas
- Formulate a compelling question about whether revolutions fulfill their promises of liberty

BNCC Skills:
- EF07HI01, EF07HI02, EF07HI04, EF07HI06

Keywords:
- Enlightenment
- revolution
- rights
- constitution
- citizenship
- sources
- liberty

Project Opportunities:
- Revolutionary documents station rotation: Declaration of Independence, Rights of Man, and Brazilian parallels
- Constitutional design lab: students draft classroom governance applying separation of powers
- Political cartoon analysis gallery with captions explaining symbolism and point of view

Assessment Suggestions:
- Comparison essay outline on American and French revolutionary causes and outcomes
- Document-based question using an Enlightenment excerpt and a revolutionary declaration
- Structured discussion rubric evaluating evidence use and respect for multiple perspectives

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
The Industrial Revolution transformed how goods were made and how people lived, creating unprecedented wealth alongside harsh labor conditions and social reform movements.

Topics:
- Origins in Britain: steam power, textile mills, coal, iron, and railroads
- Factory system, urbanization, and changing family and child labor patterns
- Capitalism and class tension; introduction to socialist and labor union responses
- Industrialization beyond Europe: United States, Japan, and late industrializers
- Environmental and geographic impacts: resource extraction, pollution, and city growth
- Brazil's industrial history: coffee export economy, early factories, and urban migration toward Rio and São Paulo

Learning Objectives:
- Explain how technological innovations accelerated industrial production and transportation
- Describe working and living conditions in early industrial cities with specific examples
- Compare owners', workers', and reformers' perspectives on industrial change
- Interpret graphs and maps showing urban growth, rail networks, and resource flows
- Connect industrialization to migration from countryside to city in Brazil and elsewhere
- Evaluate reform proposals using evidence about child labor, wages, and safety

BNCC Skills:
- EF07HI03, EF07HI17, EF07HI05

Keywords:
- Industrial Revolution
- factory
- urbanization
- labor
- capitalism
- reform
- migration

Project Opportunities:
- Industrial city map comparing Manchester, Pittsburgh, and early São Paulo growth patterns
- Worker reformer speech writing using factory inspection reports and union demands as sources
- Data project: graph industrial output or urban population change and interpret trends

Assessment Suggestions:
- Cause-and-effect chart linking inventions to social and environmental consequences
- Primary source analysis of a factory reform testimony or industrial photograph
- Short test on vocabulary and geographic spread of industrialization

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
Nationalism, imperialism, and alliance systems turned regional tensions into a global world war that destroyed empires and redrew maps.

Topics:
- Nation-state and nationalism in nineteenth-century Europe and the Americas
- New imperialism in Africa and Asia: motives, methods, and resistance
- World War I: assassination, alliances, trench warfare, and total war concepts
- Russian Revolution overview; collapse of empires and redrawing of borders
- Treaty of Versailles, League of Nations, and unresolved grievances
- Brazil in World War I: naval participation and diplomatic presence at Versailles

Learning Objectives:
- Define nationalism and explain its dual capacity to unify and divide societies
- Describe features of imperial control and identify forms of colonized peoples' resistance
- Sequence major causes and events of World War I using maps and timelines
- Analyze how total war affected civilians, economies, and political systems
- Evaluate the League of Nations' goals and structural weaknesses with sourced evidence
- Compare postwar boundary changes to contemporary conflict zones using maps

BNCC Skills:
- EF07HI10, EF07HI14, EF07HI07, EF07HI17

Keywords:
- nationalism
- imperialism
- World War I
- alliances
- treaty
- resistance
- total war

Project Opportunities:
- Alliance system simulation showing how localized crises can escalate internationally
- Imperialism case study poster on one African or Asian region with resistance leader profile
- WWI media project: annotated map of fronts plus soldier or civilian primary account

Assessment Suggestions:
- Timeline and map test on WWI causes, participants, and outcomes
- Document-based short essay on whether Versailles made lasting peace possible
- Debate on nationalism as constructive patriotism versus exclusionary ideology with evidence rules

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
World War II and the Holocaust demonstrate how extremist ideologies can devastate humanity—and why international law and human rights institutions emerged in response.

Topics:
- Great Depression's global reach; rise of fascism in Italy and Nazism in Germany
- World War II: theaters of war, home fronts, and turning points at survey level
- Holocaust: systematic persecution and genocide; importance of testimony and remembrance
- Atomic age begins; wartime conferences and foundations of the United Nations
- Universal Declaration of Human Rights and Nuremberg principles on crimes against humanity
- Brazil in World War II: FEB expedition, home front, and postwar diplomatic role; C3 capstone inquiry

Learning Objectives:
- Explain how economic crisis and propaganda enabled authoritarian regimes in Europe
- Outline major WWII events in Europe and the Pacific using maps and chronology
- Describe the Holocaust as state-organized genocide and why denial and distortion are dangerous
- Analyze the UN and UDHR as responses to war, atrocity, and colonial inequality
- Compare wartime experiences of soldiers, refugees, and marginalized groups across countries
- Conduct a capstone inquiry connecting a WWII-era issue to a present human rights challenge

BNCC Skills:

Keywords:

Project Opportunities:
- UN charter and UDHR close reading with student-generated classroom rights charter
- Holocaust remembrance project using survivor testimony or memorial design with ethical guidelines
- Capstone inquiry poster: WWII origin question, sources, claim, and civic action proposal

Assessment Suggestions:
- Map and multiple-choice test on WWII leaders, alliances, and key events
- Written response on why remembrance and education matter for preventing genocide
- Inquiry portfolio rubric covering question, sourcing, argument, and informed action components
