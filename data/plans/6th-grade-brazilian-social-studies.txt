==================================================

Grade: 6

Subject: Brazilian Social Studies

Academic Year: 2025

Overview:
Sixth-grade Brazilian Social Studies at Colégio Horizonte examines Brazil's transition from colony to nation through independence, empire, and early republic. Students analyze how slavery structured society and economy, follow the abolition movement to the Lei Áurea, and study political experiments under emperors and republican leaders. Rio de Janeiro's role as imperial capital provides a concrete lens for understanding court life, urban reform, and the tensions that shaped nineteenth-century Brazil.

Core Competencies:
- Explain causes and consequences of Brazilian independence and early nation-building
- Analyze the institution of slavery and the struggle for abolition
- Compare monarchical and republican forms of government in Brazilian history
- Evaluate primary sources about social groups excluded from full citizenship

==================================================

Unit 1

Period: First Quarter

Big Idea:
Independence movements transformed colonial Brazil into a sovereign nation with unresolved social inequalities.

Topics:
- Enlightenment and liberal ideas reaching Portuguese America
- The Portuguese court's transfer to Rio de Janeiro in 1808
- Economic crises, political disputes, and the 1822 independence process
- Key figures: Dom João VI, Dom Pedro I, and regional elites

Learning Objectives:
- Describe events leading to Brazil's independence from Portugal
- Explain why Rio de Janeiro became the center of the Portuguese Empire
- Identify social groups that supported or resisted independence
- Compare Brazilian independence with other Latin American cases
- Analyze primary sources such as proclamations and political pamphlets

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI05, EF06HI06, EF06HI08

Keywords:
- independence
- empire
- Rio de Janeiro
- monarchy
- nation
- sources

Project Opportunities:
- Timeline mural of 1808–1822 with maps of Rio and São Paulo
- Role-play council debating independence priorities and slavery
- Comparative chart of independence leaders across the Americas

Assessment Suggestions:
- Cause-and-effect diagram of independence events
- Short document analysis of the Independence Cry or related text
- Quiz on political vocabulary: colony, nation, sovereignty, constitution

==================================================

Unit 2

Period: Second Quarter

Big Idea:
The Brazilian Empire consolidated territory and institutions while relying on slavery and limited political participation.

Topics:
- First and Second Reign: Dom Pedro I and Dom Pedro II
- Imperial constitution, parliament, and provincial organization
- Coffee economy, railroads, and urban growth in Rio and São Paulo
- Wars and diplomacy: Platine region, Paraguay, and border consolidation

Learning Objectives:
- Characterize political and social organization during the Empire
- Explain economic changes that strengthened coffee elites
- Describe Rio de Janeiro's transformation as imperial capital
- Identify achievements and limits of imperial modernization projects
- Discuss how different social classes experienced empire differently

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI12, EF06HI15, EF06HI16

Keywords:
- Empire
- Dom Pedro II
- coffee
- parliament
- urbanization
- modernization

Project Opportunities:
- Imperial Rio walking tour research with historic photographs
- Mock parliamentary session on an education or infrastructure bill
- Infographic on coffee's impact on labor, trade, and city growth

Assessment Suggestions:
- Essay comparing First and Second Reign policies
- Map exercise locating imperial provinces and economic zones
- Primary source questions on imperial photographs or newspaper clips

==================================================

Unit 3

Period: Third Quarter

Big Idea:
Slavery defined Brazilian society for centuries, and its abolition resulted from resistance, law, and economic change.

Topics:
- Atlantic slave trade and the plantation system in Brazil
- Everyday resistance, quilombos, and abolitionist campaigns
- The Lei do Ventre Livre, Lei dos Sexagenários, and Lei Áurea (1888)
- Roles of enslaved people, free Black communities, and abolitionists

Learning Objectives:
- Describe how slavery organized labor, law, and social hierarchy
- Identify forms of resistance among enslaved and free Black populations
- Explain major legal steps toward abolition in chronological order
- Analyze why abolition did not automatically produce equality
- Evaluate historical arguments about slavery's legacy using evidence

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI14, EF06HI19, EF06HI21

Keywords:
- slavery
- abolition
- quilombos
- resistance
- Lei Áurea
- inequality

Project Opportunities:
- Quilombo research poster highlighting leadership and survival strategies
- Abolition timeline with biographies of José do Patrocínio, Luísa Mahin, and others
- Letter to the editor written from an 1887 abolitionist perspective

Assessment Suggestions:
- Sequencing activity on abolition laws and events
- Document-based question on an abolitionist speech or petition
- Reflection on how slavery's legacy appears in present-day Brazil

Unit 4

Period: Fourth Quarter

Big Idea:
The end of slavery and the proclamation of the republic opened new conflicts over citizenship, race, and national identity.

Topics:
- Military dissatisfaction, republican clubs, and the 1889 Proclamation
- First republic: café com leite politics, positivism, and federalism
- Indigenous policies, immigration, and nation-building projects
- Scientific racism, social Darwinism, and exclusionary ideologies

Learning Objectives:
- Explain causes of the 1889 republican coup and end of the monarchy
- Describe how the early republic distributed political power among states
- Analyze official policies toward indigenous peoples during nation-building
- Discuss how racial ideologies justified social exclusion
- Connect nineteenth-century reforms to modern citizenship debates

BNCC Skills:
- EF06HI11, EF06HI20, EF06HI23, EF06HI27

Keywords:
- republic
- citizenship
- indigenous policy
- racism
- federalism
- nation-building

Project Opportunities:
- Constitutional convention simulation drafting early republic rights
- Exhibit comparing imperial and republican symbols in Rio's public spaces
- Capstone essay linking independence, empire, abolition, and republic themes

Assessment Suggestions:
- Structured debate evaluation on republic versus monarchy arguments
- Concept map connecting slavery, abolition, and republican politics
- End-of-year exam with chronology, source analysis, and short essay sections
